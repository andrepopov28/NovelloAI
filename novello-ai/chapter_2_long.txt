It was also the first time that anyone had successfully taught a machine how to learn.

The technology behind it was a form of artificial intelligence known as deep learning, which uses neural networks – computer systems designed to mimic human brain processes – to identify patterns in data and make decisions. Deep learning algorithms were already being used in speech recognition, image identification, language translation, and many other applications. But this was the first time that they had been applied to video games.

The researchers trained their AI agent on a version of Atari 2600 game Pong called Breakout. They taught it how to play by watching thousands of hours of human gameplay videos and learning from them. A hundred years ago, industrial workers could be trained on a single assembly line task and perform that same job for decades without needing any further training.

Today, however, technology has made that model obsolete. The world of work has gone through four major phases since the Industrial Revolution:

1) **Industrialization** (1760-1900): Workers were divided into simple tasks on assembly lines.
2) **Mass Production** (1900s): This phase introduced specialization and economies of scale to increase productivity. It was driven by Ford's Model T, which made cars affordable for the first time.
3) **Information Age** (1950s-2000s): The world shifted from making physical goods to creating value through information. Personal computers, the internet, and smartphones dramatically changed how we work and communicate with each other.
4) **The Digital Transformation Era**: The fourth phase involves using technology to create new products, services, and ways of working that weren't possible before. This includes automation, artificial intelligence, blockchain, and more.

## Historical Examples

### Rosa Parks
Rosa Louise McCauley Parks was born on February 4th, 1913 in Tuskegee, Alabama. She became an African-American civil rights activist whose refusal to give up her seat on a Montgomery bus sparked the Montgomery Bus Boycott which lasted for over a year and played a significant role in ending segregation on public transportation systems nationwide. Her act of defiance has been called "the single most important event in the modern American Civil Rights Movement". Parks worked as a seamstress for nearly two decades before becoming active in politics and organizing boycotts against Jim Crow Laws. She was arrested numerous times during her involvement with other civil rights leaders including Martin Luther King Jr., who helped organize the bus boycott alongside Reverend Fred Shuttlesworth.

During this time she also founded the Montgomery Improvement Association (MIA), which organized rides for black passengers to travel around town without using the buses that were segregated. The organization also worked closely with lawyers such as Thurgood Marshall, who would later become an Associate Justice of the Supreme Court in 1967. Despite several bombings and threats against her family Parks continued her activism until federal legislation was passed outlawing discrimination based on race throughout all state transportation systems by August 29th 1956.

Rosa Louise McCauley Parks died at age 92 from heart failure due to complications with surgery in Detroit Michigan on October 24th 2005. Her legacy lives on as an icon of strength, courage and resilience during one of America’s most turbulent periods in history. She was posthumously awarded the Presidential Medal of Freedom by President George W Bush along with many other honors including Congressional Gold Medal from Congress itself. In addition she has been immortalized through various forms of media such as films such as “The Rosa Parks Story” starring Angela Bassett and books like “Rosa Parks: My Story”, written by her friend Elaine Stevens when she was 11 years old.

Parks’ story teaches us all how important it is to stand up for what we believe in even if it means facing adversity or persecution along the way. It reminds us that no matter who you are or where you come from, everyone deserves respect and equality regardless of skin color or background. As we move forward into this new era of technological advancement let’s remember those who came before us like Rosa Parks who showed us all how far determination could take us when given enough time and space to thrive.

### Steve Jobs
Steven Paul Jobs was born on February 24th, 1955 in San Francisco California but raised in Los Altos after his parents divorced. He attended Reed College for only six months before dropping out due to financial constraints while still managing to develop his passion for design and creativity which would lead him to co-found Apple Inc. with Steve Wozniak in 1976.

Jobs’ first product was the Apple I, a computer that sold for $666.66 at the time – although he himself did not attend the launch event as he had already been fired from his own company by then! Despite this setback Jobs persevered and went on to develop other revolutionary products such as the Macintosh Computer (1984) which was the first personal desktop computer designed specifically with users in mind rather than just programmers.

In 1997 Apple acquired NeXT Inc., a software development firm founded by Jobs that helped him regain control of his company after being ousted from it previously. Over the next two decades he would go on to revolutionize not only computing technology but also music players, smartphones and tablets through innovations such as iTunes Store, iPods, iPhones and iPads respectively.

Jobs had dyslexia yet still managed to earn a PhD from Zurich Polytechnic University at just 21 years old before moving onto make groundbreaking contributions in physics. His theories of relativity revolutionized our understanding of space-time and laid the groundwork for much modern scientific research.

Despite all these achievements Jobs died unexpectedly due to pancreatic cancer on October 5th 2011 leaving behind his wife Laurene Powell-Jobs, three daughters and an enduring legacy as one of America’s most influential entrepreneurs. He taught us that no matter what life throws at you, if you truly believe in yourself then anything is possible! ### Albert Einstein
Albert Einstein was a German-born physicist who made significant contributions to theoretical physics along with his theories of relativity. He had dyslexia yet still managed to earn a PhD from Zurich Polytechnic University at just 21 years old before moving on to make groundbreaking contributions in physics that revolutionized our understanding of space-time and laid the groundwork for much modern scientific research.

Einstein’s most famous theory was general relativity which states that gravity causes objects with mass to curve spacetime, affecting the motion of nearby objects. This idea helped explain phenomena such as Mercury’s precession around the sun and black holes among other things. He also developed special relativity which explains how space contracts and time dilates when an object accelerates past certain speeds.

As well as being a renowned scientist Einstein was also an outspoken pacifist who advocated nuclear disarmament and promoted world peace through dialogue rather than violence throughout his lifetime. In fact he even won the Nobel Prize in Physics for his explanation of the photoelectric effect which laid the groundwork for quantum mechanics later on down the line!

Despite these achievements Einstein died at age 76 due to an abdominal aortic aneurysm but left behind a legacy as one of history’s greatest minds and role models for those who want to achieve greatness despite adversity. His story teaches us all how important it is to pursue our passions even when faced with obstacles along the way. It reminds us that no matter who you are or where you come from, everyone deserves respect and equality regardless of skin color or background. As we move forward into this new era of technological advancement let’s remember those who came before us like Albert Einstein who showed us all how far determination could take us when given enough time and space to thrive.

In his book, Tim Ferriss details a simple yet effective process for making decisions called “The Five Second Rule” which involves asking yourself five questions before taking action. The questions are:

1. What’s the worst thing that could happen?
2. What’s the best case scenario?
3. How much time do you have to act?
4. Is this really worth it?
5. Are you overthinking it?

These simple questions help Tim to make decisions quickly and confidently without second guessing himself or letting fear dictate his choices.

The principle of human leverage is similar in that it allows us to take action faster, smarter, and with more conviction than before. By leveraging our time, resources, and knowledge we can achieve outsized results compared to what would be possible without the help of technology and automation.
