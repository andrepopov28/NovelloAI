The Story and Hook
We are living through a historic transformation, but few recognize it yet. We have entered the era of exponential technologies that will enable the first '100x Humans'. But most people do not realize what this means.

Consider what we take for granted today - the mobile phone. It took 50 years to reach a billion users after the invention of the cell phone in 1973, but it only took 10 more years to add another billion after 2010 when smartphones started proliferating at scale. This trend is accelerating as we move into AI and robotics. AI will transform how humans work, enabling us to do much more with a lot less effort. Just like the mobile phone, it too has an adoption curve where it took 50 years to reach the first million users in 2012, but then it only took 10 years to add another million after 2020.

We are on the cusp of this transformation today. But few recognize it yet. Most people still think we live in a linear world. They have not realized that technology is now exponentially accelerating us into a new age of superhumans.

This is what my book, 'Titan Inc.', is about - explaining how we can become '100x Humans' through the power of exponential technologies. This will be the greatest transformation humanity has ever seen. We are on the cusp. But few recognize it yet.

Labor is what transforms raw materials into products and services; it is what allows us to work together toward shared goals. But if we look at economic history, we can see that even as human productivity increased over time, so too did the size of our population. This meant that in a sense, we were "leaving" the economy - producing more goods than we could consume.
But what happens when technology allows us to produce even more? When we can transform raw materials into products and services at an unprecedented scale? We are no longer just leaving the economy; we're creating a new one. And this is what I call human leverage.

## Historical and Modern Examples

 When I consider historical examples of technological progress outstripping economic growth, I can't help but think about the Industrial Revolution. In just 50 years between 1820 and 1870, per capita income in Britain grew fivefold while population doubled – meaning that per person consumption rose by a factor of ten! That's a truly remarkable transformation.

But as we've seen with Moore's Law, this progress is far from linear. The growth rate has been slowing since the early 1970s and may be approaching its limit. And if AI can help automate more of our labor, then it seems plausible that per capita income could continue growing at a fast pace for decades to come.

In this chapter, we'll explore some modern examples where technological progress is already outpacing economic growth: automated vehicles, renewable energy, and healthcare diagnostics. These are all areas where AI can help us do things better and faster than ever before. But as we've seen in the past, these advances don't always lead to a commensurate boost in GDP or standard of living.

The key lesson from history is that when technological progress accelerates, it doesn't always benefit everyone evenly. The rise of 100x humans means there will be winners and losers – those who embrace automation and become more productive, and those who fall behind as their labor becomes obsolete.

So far we've looked at a few possible scenarios for how AI could impact the future of work. But before diving into specific predictions, let's consider some historical examples where technological progress has outpaced economic growth:

- The Agricultural Revolution (10th century BC–500 AD) led to an 8-fold increase in global population without much rise in GDP per capita.

- The Industrial Revolution (1760s–1840s) saw a fivefold boost in British income levels over just 50 years. However, this progress was not evenly distributed: factory workers suffered from long hours and poor working conditions.

- The Information Age (1971-present) has seen productivity growth outstrip GDP gains due to automation of routine tasks like word processing and spreadsheets.

These examples show that while technological progress can increase overall economic output, it's not guaranteed to raise living standards for everyone. In the next chapter we'll explore how AI could shape our future of work in more detail by looking at specific sectors such as transportation and healthcare.

## The Principle Applied

 As we've discussed at length, the death of smart is no longer something confined solely to technology or economics – it's becoming an unavoidable reality for every aspect of our lives. The principle applied here is quite simple – just as a 10x increase in computing power will not be enough to compensate for Moore's Law slowing down, so too will a 2x increase in human productivity not be enough to keep up with the new economy of 100x humans.

And yet, despite this inevitable reality, many still cling to old ways. We see it in political debates where candidates continue to advocate for policies that ignore technological progress and its economic consequences. We see it in businesses clinging desperately to old models while refusing to adapt to changing markets. We see it in everyday life as people resist the adoption of new technologies or fail to take advantage of tools like automation and AI to improve their lives.

The reality is that we are at a crossroads, where our very future depends on whether we embrace change – not just technologically, but also economically, socially, and spiritually. The choice may seem difficult now, but it's one that will determine the fate of our civilization for generations to come.
